The demand for websites, especially from small local businesses, remains consistently high. While AI tools and no-code platforms (like Wix ADI, Framer, Webflow) have simplified basic site creation, many businesses still require custom solutions, professional guidance, and ongoing maintenance. The key to success is specializing in a niche (e.g., dentists, local restaurants, fitness studios) and delivering value faster than larger agencies or generalist freelancers.

Key Takeaway: Don't compete on price with template sites. Position yourself as a specialist solving specific business problems. Focus on building relationships and showcasing tangible results (e.g., more leads, better online presence) for your clients.

⚠️ Top 3 Risks & How to Avoid Them

1. Undervaluing Your Work (High Risk)

Problem: Charging too little, especially when starting out. This leads to burnout and attracts low-quality clients.
Fix: Research competitor pricing for your niche. Package your services (e.g., "Basic Business Presence," "Lead Generation Site") rather than charging hourly. Price for value, not just time.

2. Poor Client Communication (Moderate Risk)

Problem: Misunderstandings about scope, timelines, and expectations can derail projects and damage your reputation.
Fix: Use clear contracts. Set expectations upfront. Provide regular updates. Establish a clear feedback loop. Use project management tools (e.g., Trello, Asana).

3. Scope Creep (Moderate Risk)

Problem: Clients constantly asking for "just one more small thing" outside the agreed-upon scope, eating into your profit and time.
Fix: Define a clear project scope in your contract. Implement a formal change request process for any new features, with associated costs and timelines.

🚀 Step-by-Step Launch Plan

1

Define Your Niche & Offer (Day 1-7)

Don't be a generalist. Choose 1-2 types of local businesses (e.g., cafes, plumbers, salons). Research their specific website needs and pain points. Create a clear service package (e.g., "Starter Website for Cafes") with a fixed price.

2

Build Your Portfolio & Showcase (Week 1-3)

Create 2-3 high-quality dummy websites for your chosen niche. These serve as your portfolio. Get a simple portfolio website for yourself. Request testimonials if you have past work.

3

Find Your First Clients (Month 1-3)

Start with local outreach. Visit businesses in person, send personalized emails, leverage LinkedIn. Offer a small discount for your first few clients in exchange for testimonials. Focus on solving their immediate problems.

4

Systemize & Scale (Month 4+)

As you get clients, document your processes. Use templates for contracts, proposals, and designs. Consider offering maintenance packages for recurring revenue. Explore partnerships with local marketing agencies.
