ASSET 1: TWITTER/X THREAD
==========================

1/ I built a $70k micro-SaaS in just 6 days of actual work.

No VC funding. No big team. Just one problem, solved fast.

Here's the full playbook 🧵

2/ The idea came from a friend who needed a simple JSON viewer for non-technical users.

That's it. Not a platform. Not a suite of tools. One tiny problem that a specific audience would pay to solve.

3/ I built the first version in 2 days (12 hours).

Vue JS frontend. Stripe Checkout. Netlify hosting. Free.

One-time payment of $5. Shipped. Done.

4/ The "complex backend" instinct will kill your momentum.

My users were non-technical — if they knew how to edit frontend code, they wouldn't need my app.

Frontend-only was the right call. Know your audience.

5/ Analytics weren't vanity metrics. They were survival tools.

When sales dropped to zero, Google Analytics told me exactly why: the JSON structure had changed upstream.

We lost 3 weeks. That hurt. Now I log everything.

6/ Promotion was two things: Google Keyword Planner + a YouTube tutorial.

Targeted the exact search terms our users were typing. Video took off. Killed the ad spend entirely.

7/ The 5-step micro-SaaS framework:

→ Find ONE niche, ONE problem
→ Validate in communities (Reddit, Discord, Twitter)
→ Confirm someone's already searching for it
→ Ship fast, iterate on the 20% that drives 80% of value
→ Use the simplest stack that serves your audience

8/ $70k. 14 months on autopilot. 6 days of work.

The question isn't "is it possible?"

It's: are people ready to pay for the problem you solve?

Start there. Build second.

ASSET 2: LINKEDIN POST
=======================

$70k from 6 days of work. Here's what most founders miss about this story.

An indie hacker built a micro-SaaS in 2019 — a simple JSON data viewer. Non-technical users needed to read exported app data; he gave them a clean UI for $5 a pop. 6,000+ sales. 14 months of autopilot revenue.

What's worth stealing from this:

1. The problem was laughably small. That was the point. One specific pain point, one specific audience. Not a platform — a tool.

2. He matched the tech to the user. His customers weren't developers, so a complex backend would've been overkill and slower to build. Frontend-only was the right answer.

3. Analytics weren't a dashboard — they were an early warning system. When the source app changed its JSON structure, sales flatlined. He only caught it because he was logging everything.

4. The YouTube video beat paid ads. Two tutorials (English + French) targeting exact user search terms eventually made Google Ads unnecessary.

The real lesson: speed of execution matters less than clarity of problem. He shipped fast because he knew *exactly* what he was building and *exactly* who it was for.

What's the one problem your users have that you keep deprioritising because it feels too small?
