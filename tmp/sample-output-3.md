VCs talk about a lot of things when evaluating SaaS companies — growth rate, TAM, team, product.

But there's one metric that moves the valuation needle more than all of them: **Net Revenue Retention (NRR)**.

Here's why it matters.

**The math is simple:**

NRR measures how much revenue you generate from *existing* customers (through expansion, upsells, cross-sells) minus any churn or contraction.

100% NRR = you kept your customers but didn't grow revenue from them  
120% NRR = existing customers generated 20% *more* revenue this year without new acquisitions

That 20% difference? It's worth **20-30% more in valuation**. We're talking tens of millions of dollars.

**Here's the compounding magic:**

A company with 120% NRR doesn't just grow 20% faster. Over 10 years, that 20% compounds into **6.2x growth**. A company with 130% NRR hits **13.8x**.

Meanwhile, a company chasing new logos at 100% NRR grows linearly. It stays flat.

**Why this matters for your fundraise:**

Investors don't just look at ARR. They look at *how* you're growing it.

Two companies hitting $38M ARR in Year 5 look identical on a slide. But:

- Company A (100% NRR) acquired $11M+ in new ARR Year 5 to hit that growth
- Company B (120% NRR) only acquired $5.5M in new ARR — customers did the other $5.5M through expansion

Same result. Company B is **half as expensive to scale**.

That's capital efficiency. That's defensibility. That's a premium valuation.

**How to improve your NRR:**

1. **Upsell** — Tier your product and let customers upgrade as they grow
2. **Cross-sell** — Build complementary features that expand wallet share
3. **Increase usage** — Make the product so sticky and valuable that customers use it more and depend on it

The founders building the next generation of billion-dollar companies aren't grinding on new customer acquisition. They're obsessing over expansion revenue.

If you're not measuring NRR, you're not optimizing for it.
